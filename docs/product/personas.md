# Verify Me -- User Personas

---

## 1. Lakshmi -- Domestic Worker and Cook

### Demographics

- **Age:** 35
- **Location:** Whitefield, Bangalore (lives in nearby slum settlement, works in gated apartment communities)
- **Education:** Completed 8th standard (middle school)
- **Languages:** Kannada (native), Hindi (conversational), English (minimal -- can recognize common words)
- **Income:** INR 12,000-18,000/month across 3-4 households
- **Family:** Married, two school-age children
- **Phone:** Redmi 9A (Android 10, 2GB RAM), prepaid plan with 1.5 GB/day data

### Goals

- Find stable, well-paying households to work for without depending on a middleman or agency that takes a cut.
- Build a reputation that follows her. She has been working as a cook and house cleaner for 12 years, but none of that history is documented.
- Protect herself from unfair accusations. Two years ago, she was falsely accused of theft in a WhatsApp group and lost three households overnight. She had no way to defend herself.
- Save time searching for new work when a family moves away or no longer needs help.

### Frustrations

- The WhatsApp blacklisting incident still affects her. Some apartment security guards recognize her name and turn her away.
- Agencies take INR 2,000-5,000 as placement fees and do not help if there is a dispute.
- She spent INR 1,500 on police verification for one employer, and the process took six weeks. The next employer wanted a fresh one.
- Apps she has tried (Urban Company, etc.) treat her as a gig worker with per-task pricing. She wants long-term household employment, not one-off cleaning jobs.

### Tech Comfort

- **Level:** Low-to-moderate.
- Uses WhatsApp daily (voice messages, photos, video calls with family). Can navigate YouTube.
- Struggles with English-only interfaces. Abandons apps that require typing long forms.
- Comfortable with OTP-based login (uses it for Paytm).
- Has never used DigiLocker but has an Aadhaar card (physical copy).
- Prefers voice notes over typing.

### Trust Concerns

- Worried about "the government" tracking her through Aadhaar. Needs reassurance about what data is stored and who can see it.
- Fears that incident reports will be one-sided -- the employer's word against hers.
- Wants to know: "If I register, can an employer use this to fire me more easily?"

### Key Scenarios

1. **Registration:** Lakshmi's neighbor tells her about Verify Me. She downloads the app and registers using her phone number. The Hindi interface guides her through selecting "Cook" and "House Cleaning" as her services, and she picks her area from a list. Total time: 4 minutes.

2. **Verification:** The app explains DigiLocker in simple Hindi with illustrations. Lakshmi taps through the DigiLocker consent flow. Her identity is verified. She feels proud seeing the green "Verified" badge on her Trust Card.

3. **Showing her Trust Card:** At a new apartment, the hirer asks for references. Lakshmi opens the app and shows her Trust Card. The hirer scans the QR code and sees verified identity, 8 months on platform, zero incidents. Lakshmi is hired.

4. **Responding to an incident:** A hirer reports that Lakshmi did not show up for two days without notice. Lakshmi receives an SMS alert. She opens the app and records a voice note explaining that she was in the hospital (her son was sick) and she had informed the employer's husband. The admin reviews both accounts.

## 2. Rajesh -- Driver

### Demographics

- **Age:** 28
- **Location:** Gurgaon, Haryana (rents a room near MG Road, drives in DLF and Golf Course Road areas)
- **Education:** Completed 12th standard (high school)
- **Languages:** Hindi (native), English (basic -- can read road signs, app interfaces)
- **Income:** INR 20,000-28,000/month (combination of a regular family and ad-hoc driving)
- **Family:** Single, sends money to parents in Bihar
- **Phone:** Samsung Galaxy M13 (Android 12, 4GB RAM), postpaid plan with 2 GB/day data

### Goals

- Build a verified driving record that he can show to potential employers. He has a clean driving record and commercial license but no way to prove his reliability.
- Get hired by families in premium apartments where employers pay INR 15,000-20,000/month for a dedicated driver.
- Differentiate himself from drivers who have had accidents or traffic violations.
- Eventually save enough to buy his own car and start a small rental business.

### Frustrations

- Gets treated with suspicion by new employers. "They check my license, ask for references, but they have no way to know if I am safe."
- Lost a well-paying job when the family relocated to Mumbai. Had to start from scratch in Gurgaon with no local references.
- Ola/Uber pays INR 15,000-20,000/month after expenses for 12+ hour days. He wants a personal driver role with better pay and fixed hours.
- One employer accused him of overcharging on fuel. He had no way to formally dispute this -- the employer simply told the RWA (Resident Welfare Association) secretary.

### Tech Comfort

- **Level:** Moderate.
- Uses Google Maps daily for navigation, Paytm for payments, YouTube, Instagram.
- Comfortable downloading and navigating apps in English and Hindi.
- Has used DigiLocker once to store his driving license.
- Can fill out basic forms on a phone but prefers short forms with dropdowns.

### Trust Concerns

- Wants to know if employers can see his home address or personal phone number before he agrees to share.
- Concerned about competitors filing fake incident reports to damage his Trust Card.
- Wants to understand: "If I have one incident, does that mean no one will hire me?"

### Key Scenarios

1. **Registration and verification:** Rajesh registers in 3 minutes, selects "Driver" as his service, and completes DigiLocker verification on the first try using his existing DigiLocker account. He adds his commercial driving license as an additional credential.

2. **Trust Card sharing:** A family in DLF Phase 5 is looking for a driver. They find Rajesh through the app search. He receives a consent request and approves it. The family sees his verified identity, 14 months on platform, zero incidents, and hires him for a trial week.

3. **Building trust over time:** After one year on the platform, Rajesh's Trust Card shows "12 months incident-free." He uses this to negotiate a higher salary with a new employer.

## 3. Priya -- Apartment Community Manager (Hirer)

### Demographics

- **Age:** 40
- **Location:** Prestige Lakeside Habitat, Whitefield, Bangalore (community of 3,500+ apartments)
- **Education:** MBA from a mid-tier business school
- **Languages:** English (primary), Hindi, Kannada (basic)
- **Occupation:** Volunteer RWA (Resident Welfare Association) general secretary; previously worked in HR at an IT company
- **Income:** Upper-middle class household (combined INR 3-4 lakh/month)
- **Phone:** iPhone 14, high-speed broadband at home

### Goals

- Establish a standardized system for verifying domestic workers entering the apartment community. Currently, the 3,500-unit complex has no unified process.
- Replace the chaotic WhatsApp-group-based system where residents post warnings about workers with no verification or due process.
- Reduce the RWA's liability. Two incidents last year (a theft and a case of verbal abuse) resulted in threats of legal action against the RWA for "allowing unverified staff."
- Make the process fair for workers. Priya is personally uncomfortable with the WhatsApp blacklisting culture and believes it is both unjust and potentially illegal.

### Frustrations

- The current system is either "trust everyone" or "trust no one." There is no middle ground.
- Police verification is expensive, slow, and residents complain about the cost and delay.
- Multiple residents maintain personal "blacklists" of workers, shared in WhatsApp groups with no evidence standards. Some are based on misunderstandings or prejudice.
- The RWA has no legal framework for what they can or cannot do regarding worker verification.
- She has tried BookMyBai and similar services, but they only cover placement -- not ongoing trust or incident management.

### Tech Comfort

- **Level:** High.
- Manages RWA communications via WhatsApp groups, NoBroker community app, email.
- Comfortable with web dashboards and admin panels.
- Expects professional-grade UX and fast response times.

### Trust Concerns

- Needs assurance that the platform is legally compliant -- the RWA cannot be seen endorsing a system that violates privacy laws.
- Wants to understand the incident review process in detail. "What if a resident files a false report? What if a substantiated report leads the worker to sue the RWA?"
- Concerned about adoption: "Will workers actually sign up? Will residents use it instead of WhatsApp?"

### Key Scenarios

1. **Community onboarding:** Priya registers the apartment community on Verify Me. She presents it at an RWA meeting as a replacement for WhatsApp-based worker alerts. She shares the hirer signup link with 200 active RWA WhatsApp group members.

2. **Worker verification at gate:** A new domestic worker arrives at the community gate. The security guard asks for her Verify Me Trust Card. The worker shows the QR code. The guard scans it and sees a verified trust profile. Entry is logged.

3. **Incident reporting:** A resident reports a theft to Priya. Instead of posting in the WhatsApp group, Priya guides them to file a structured incident report through Verify Me. The worker is notified and given a chance to respond before any action is taken.

## 4. Amit -- Individual Hirer (Young Professional)

### Demographics

- **Age:** 30
- **Location:** Powai, Mumbai (rents a 2BHK apartment)
- **Education:** Engineering degree, works as a product manager at a fintech company
- **Languages:** English (primary), Hindi
- **Income:** INR 2 lakh/month
- **Family:** Married, one infant child
- **Phone:** iPhone 15 Pro

### Goals

- Find a trustworthy nanny/caretaker for his 8-month-old daughter. This is his highest priority and most anxiety-inducing hiring decision.
- Have some form of verified background check that goes beyond a phone call to "the previous employer's number that the nanny herself provided."
- Ensure ongoing accountability -- not just a one-time check at hiring, but a system where concerns can be raised and addressed.

### Frustrations

- Spent three weeks and contacted 15 people to find a nanny. Word of mouth in a city where he has lived for only two years is limited.
- The agency he contacted charged INR 8,000 as placement fee and provided a "police verified" nanny, but the verification document was a photocopy of unclear provenance.
- He and his wife both work. Leaving an infant with someone whose background they cannot meaningfully verify causes constant anxiety.
- He has looked at nanny-cam solutions but finds them invasive and insufficient -- they record problems after they happen, not prevent them.

### Tech Comfort

- **Level:** Very high.
- Product manager at a tech company. Evaluates apps professionally.
- Will notice UX issues, slow load times, and privacy policy gaps.
- Expects clear data handling disclosures.

### Trust Concerns

- Wants maximum transparency: "What exactly has been verified? What does 'identity verified' actually mean technically?"
- Concerned about the cold-start problem: "If a nanny just joined, her Trust Card is blank. How is that useful?"
- Wants to know what recourse he has if something goes wrong. "If I report an incident, what actually happens?"

### Key Scenarios

1. **Searching for a nanny:** Amit searches for "nanny" in the Powai area. He finds 12 results. He filters for "Identity Verified" and sees 7. He sends consent requests to 3 workers whose profiles match his needs.

2. **Evaluating a Trust Card:** One nanny, Sunita, has been on the platform for 11 months with zero incidents. Another, Geeta, has 3 months and one incident marked "unsubstantiated." Amit reads the Trust Card details and decides to interview both, feeling more informed than he would with word of mouth alone.

3. **Filing a report:** After two months, Amit notices that the nanny has been arriving consistently late. He files a structured report under "no-show/abandonment" and describes the pattern. The nanny receives the report and responds, explaining that her bus route changed. They resolve it informally, and Amit withdraws the report.

## 5. Suresh -- Electrician

### Demographics

- **Age:** 45
- **Location:** Peenya, Bangalore (lives in a rented house, works across North Bangalore)
- **Education:** ITI diploma in electrician trade
- **Languages:** Kannada (native), Hindi (conversational), English (cannot read or write)
- **Income:** INR 15,000-25,000/month (variable, depends on calls)
- **Family:** Married, three children (one in college)
- **Phone:** Recently migrated from a JioPhone (feature phone) to a Redmi 10 (Android 11, 4GB RAM) six months ago. Still learning smartphone basics.

### Goals

- Get more work calls from apartment communities. Currently relies on word of mouth and a few regular clients.
- Stop losing jobs to younger electricians who are listed on Urban Company. He is more experienced but has no online presence.
- Build a verified credential that shows his ITI certification and 20 years of experience.
- Not get scammed. He has heard stories of apps that charge workers for listings or take a cut of their earnings.

### Frustrations

- Has no idea how to "build a profile online." His son helped him create a JustDial listing two years ago, but he gets mostly spam calls from it.
- Urban Company requires him to work at their rates (INR 300-500 per visit), which is less than what he charges regular clients (INR 500-1,500 depending on the job).
- Cannot read English-only interfaces. Even many Hindi apps use complex language that does not match his spoken Hindi.
- Lost a regular apartment community client when the RWA changed and the new secretary brought in "her own electrician." No record of his years of service there.

### Tech Comfort

- **Level:** Low.
- Can make calls, use WhatsApp (voice messages only), and watch YouTube videos.
- Cannot type in Hindi on the phone keyboard. His son handles anything that requires typing.
- Gets confused by multi-step processes and modal dialogs.
- Understands the concept of OTP (uses it for Paytm) but sometimes misses the SMS and has to retry.

### Trust Concerns

- "Will this app charge me money?" -- His primary concern. Free-to-use is essential for worker adoption.
- "Will my Aadhaar information be safe?" -- Has heard news stories about Aadhaar data leaks.
- Does not fully understand what a "Trust Card" is, but understands the concept of "a good name" (reputation) that he can show to new clients.

### Key Scenarios

1. **Assisted registration:** Suresh's son helps him download the app and register. The Kannada interface (when available) or Hindi interface with simple language helps Suresh understand each step. He selects "Electrician" from a visual icon-based service picker. His son helps with DigiLocker verification.

2. **Showing Trust Card:** When an apartment resident calls Suresh for electrical work, he shows his Trust Card on his phone. The green verified badge and "18 months, no incidents" gives the resident confidence. Suresh does not need to understand the technology -- he just shows the card.

3. **Receiving an incident report:** A resident reports that Suresh overcharged for a repair. Suresh receives an SMS (not just a push notification -- he might miss push notifications). His son helps him open the app, read the report, and record a voice note explaining his pricing. The admin reviews and finds the charge was within market range. Report is marked unsubstantiated.

## 6. Meera -- Staffing Agency Owner

### Demographics

- **Age:** 38
- **Location:** HSR Layout, Bangalore
- **Education:** Bachelor of Commerce, plus various business management short courses
- **Languages:** English, Hindi, Kannada
- **Occupation:** Founder of "HomeStaff Solutions," a placement agency for domestic workers serving South Bangalore
- **Income:** INR 4-6 lakh/month (business revenue)
- **Team:** 3 employees (coordinator, field recruiter, accounts)
- **Phone:** iPhone 13, also uses a laptop for business operations
- **Portfolio:** 200+ active workers on her rolls, serves 500+ households

### Goals

- Reduce the cost and time of worker verification. Currently spends INR 800-1,200 per worker on police verification, which takes 3-6 weeks and is often outdated by the time it arrives.
- Differentiate her agency from the dozens of WhatsApp-based placement operators who do no verification at all.
- Reduce liability. She has been threatened with legal action twice when workers placed through her agency were involved in incidents. She needs a system that demonstrates due diligence.
- Scale her business to Mumbai without physically being there.

### Frustrations

- Police verification is her single biggest operational bottleneck. She cannot place workers until verification clears, and families are impatient.
- No standardized way to track worker history across placements. She maintains an Excel sheet with worker names, placement history, and notes -- but it is incomplete and not auditable.
- Workers frequently "jump" agencies -- they work through her for one placement, then go directly to the client for the next one. She has no way to maintain a relationship beyond the initial placement.
- The DPDP Act 2023 has made her nervous. She stores Aadhaar copies, address proofs, and photos of 200+ workers in Google Drive folders. She knows this is probably not compliant but does not know what to do about it.

### Tech Comfort

- **Level:** High.
- Uses Google Workspace, WhatsApp Business, and basic CRM tools.
- Comfortable with web dashboards and would expect an agency-facing portal.
- Evaluates tools by ROI -- "Will this save me time and money versus my current process?"

### Trust Concerns

- Views Verify Me as potentially both an ally and a competitor. "If workers can verify themselves, do they still need my agency?"
- Wants API access to verify workers through the platform rather than duplicate effort.
- Concerned about data ownership: "If I onboard my 200 workers, does the platform own the relationship?"
- Needs clear terms on how incident data is shared with agencies vs. individual hirers.

### Key Scenarios

1. **Evaluating the platform:** Meera learns about Verify Me from an apartment RWA she works with. She registers as a hirer and evaluates the product. She is interested in using it to replace her police verification process for new workers.

2. **Onboarding workers:** Meera organizes a registration drive at her office. She helps 50 workers create accounts and complete DigiLocker verification in a single day. Workers who are already on the platform are linked to her agency (with their consent).

3. **Using Trust Cards for placement:** When a family requests a cook, Meera shares Trust Cards of three verified workers. The family can see verified identity, time on platform, and incident history. This replaces Meera's previous practice of sharing photocopied Aadhaar cards and police verification letters.

4. **Future: Agency portal:** In Phase 2, Meera wants a portal where she can manage her worker roster, track placements, and view aggregate trust data -- without accessing individual worker details beyond what they have consented to share.
